The Two-Letter Words Are a Weapon

Not a list to recite — the connective tissue that lets every other play happen.

Today's win: understand why the 107 two-letter words are the highest-return study in Scrabble, then drill your recognition of the trickiest ones until it's instant. You said you already know you need these — good instinct. This lesson makes sure you learn them as a tool, not trivia.

Why these 107 tiny words matter

Beginners think two-letter words are for scoring two points when you're stuck. Strong players know they're the reason you can score anywhere. They do three jobs:

  1. They unlock parallel plays. When you lay a word right alongside an existing one, every touching pair of letters must itself be a legal word. Knowing the 2s instantly tells you which stacks are legal — that's where the big scores hide.
  2. They fit where nothing else does. Late in the game the board is cramped. A one- or two-tile play through a premium square is often your best move — and needs a legal 2-letter word to exist.
  3. They cash in your heavy tiles. QI and ZA drop a 10- or 5-point tile onto a bonus square. JO, OX, XU do the same. These few words win real games.
See job #1 in action The board already has DO. You play OX directly beneath it:
DO OX
Reading down the columns you've also made DO and OX. One little play, three scoring words — but only because you knew OX is legal. Miss that, and you never see the move.

The one skill to build today

You don't need to recall all 107 cold yet. First build recognition: shown any two letters, instantly know valid-or-not. The hard part isn't the obvious ones (IN, TO) — it's the strange-but-legal ones (QI, HM, XU, UT) and the plausible-but-illegal ones. Watch for these NWL2023 traps — they're legal in the international (Collins) list but not ours:

GU
KO

Red = not valid in NWL2023. These trip up players who learned from international word lists or apps set to Collins.

Drill: valid or not? (retrieval practice)

No hints, no peeking at the sheet. Tap your answer; you'll get instant feedback and the meaning of each real word. Miss one and it comes back later in the round. Aim for 90%.

Why a quiz and not just re-reading the list? Because effortful recall is what builds long-term memory. Reading feels productive but fades fast; retrieving from memory — even getting it wrong — is what makes it stick. This is the core method we'll use throughout the course.

Your daily habit (5 min) Keep the 107 Two-Letter Words sheet handy. Once a day, run this drill or read one letter-group aloud with its meaning. A few minutes daily beats one long cram — spacing is what moves these from "I recognize it" to "I own it." In a week or two, all 107 are automatic.
